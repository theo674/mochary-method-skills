# Accountability: Full Reference

Source: Mochary Method (Matt Mochary)

## 1. Agreement Tracker

Doc: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1QzGvP6MrmlD6YVilTiNjEsPgRRvjzvWrqGXKSz_c68k

### Why You Need One

Under ~20 people in one space, you can overhear priorities and obstacles. Once remote or >20 people, you need a structured written system where agreements are tracked to completion. Without it, team members work on wrong priorities and the CEO can't unblock people.

### Key Principles

- Each person needs ONE location to see all actions they've agreed to do
- Use tools like Asana, Monday.com, ClickUp, Notion, Coda (not proliferating Google Docs)
- Only track actions agreed upon with others. Use a personal tool (Things3, Omnifocus, etc.) for solo to-do items
- **Never blindly assign tasks.** Only the action-doer writes the task. They'll never write something they didn't agree to.

### Rollout Sequence

1. Use it yourself first
2. Use in your 1-1s with direct reports
3. Use in your team meeting (this is where it becomes powerful — transparency boosts morale)
4. Require directs to cascade to their 1-1s and team meetings
5. Continue until company-wide

### Why Transparency Boosts Morale

Until people can see what others are doing, each person doubts that anyone else is doing real work. Everyone thinks they're the only one pulling weight. Visible tracking instantly fixes this.

### Exception

Engineering teams may use Jira instead. That's fine.

## 2. DRIs: Directly-Responsible Individuals

Doc: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1I4jBy1XryEdknFoDCMUY93PEn0rR-oOOx6yiLSiaa38

### Core Principle

"We have many projects and metrics that no one is directly responsible for, and therefore we get neither reporting nor action on them."

A DRI is needed for every: **Action, Metric, Goal, Project, Meeting, Decision.**

The DRI is responsible for reporting status AND pushing toward the goal line.

### Common Pushbacks and Answers

**"Junior people don't have authority to compel action."**
→ Someone as DRI is better than no one. If they can't influence, they Clean Escalate to the Apex Manager.

**"DRIs will over-optimize for their thing at the expense of the whole."**
→ True, but the bigger problem is apathy without DRIs. Address through Clean Escalations from affected parties.

**"Junior DRIs will make bad decisions."**
→ The DRI reports regularly (what they did, current status, what they plan to do). The Manager is the Decision-Maker on overall direction. Like a Recruiter (DRI for finding candidates) vs Hiring Manager (decides who gets the offer).

### Project DRI Rules

- Don't allocate resources until intended impact is defined and a DRI is assigned
- Regularly review and ruthlessly kill low-priority or failing projects
- Set cadences: reporting (likely monthly), live/die decisions (likely quarterly)

## 3. Clean Escalation

Doc: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1mDxWPYirGOvs1MxdCkI98U-_7lNjr_b10jclR_D-laQ

### The Problem

"People come to me and complain about others." Cross-department conflicts (Marketing vs Sales, Product vs Engineering) lead to "dirty escalations" where you hear only one side.

### Dirty vs Clean

- **Dirty:** One party comes to CEO alone, complains, demands action
- **Clean:** Both parties present prepared positions to the Apex Manager simultaneously

### The Process

1. Person A has a problem with Person B
2. A goes to B first, states the problem and proposed solution
3. If they agree → done
4. If they can't agree → agree to Clean Escalate to their **Apex Manager** (the manager with hire/fire authority over both)
5. Both jointly request a meeting with Apex Manager
6. Both prepare written positions, submitted before the meeting
7. At the meeting: all read both documents, Apex Manager asks questions, then decides
8. If Apex Manager needs more info: lists exactly what info is needed and who collects it, sets new meeting. **One mulligan only** — must decide at the second meeting

### Critical Rule

To ensure clean escalations happen: **say "no" to all dirty escalations.** Once you do, your company and life become cleaner.

## 4. First 2 Minutes of Actions

Doc: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1Cru5o_o9ZgQPRD3XI41fcFLO0ToQyhP17fv7MUW_UG4

Inspired by Frank Slootman's "Bias to Action" from *Amp It Up*.

### Core Idea

The hardest part of any task is starting. Begin the first 2 minutes of every action during the meeting. Reserve the last 10 minutes of every meeting for this.

### Two Types of Actions

**Actions you do yourself:**
- < 2 minutes → do it immediately
- \> 2 minutes → estimate time needed, create a calendar event with the action as title

**Actions involving someone else:**
- Already have a meeting scheduled → add to that meeting's agenda
- No meeting: message takes < 2 min → send it now
- No meeting: message takes > 2 min → schedule calendar time to write it

### Why It Works

Immediate momentum. Starting makes follow-through easier. Small beginnings create self-sustaining progress.

## 5. Areas of Responsibility (AORs)

Doc: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1v72Mozz6Zr-3Knzcd9yNcs1E8-QKk5NkTVmR6pIQj98

### Core Principle

"Tragedy of the commons." When several people share responsibility, the action doesn't get done. Group tasks into categories and assign each to one (and only one) person.

### Implementation

Create a document listing every function in the company with the responsible person. This serves as both a company directory and ensures no functions fall through cracks.

### No Single Point of Failure

For every function:
1. **Write down all processes** — when you do something for the second time, document the steps
2. **Cross-train a backup** for each role — have the backup co-work with the primary until they know the role

### Rollout (45 min at Exec Team meeting or Offsite)

1. [15 min] CEO shares their AOR example. All execs write their AORs in real-time
2. [15 min] All review each other's, identify conflicts (resolve in 2 min or create IPS for later)
3. [15 min] Each exec writes one process doc and links it to their AOR
4. Each exec cascades to their teams: complete AOR list + one process per week

## 6. OKR Creation

Doc: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1GoiH4pwsph5rwSM_wMMiMJkX6-qFVwO0SVYkodNZhwg

### Structure: 3 and 3

3 Objectives, 3 Key Results each. Create at company → department → team → individual level.

**Warning:** Don't create department OKRs without company OKRs first. This leads to siloed fiefdoms.

### Definitions

- **Objective (O):** "Where do we want to go?" — inspiring like a movie tagline, doesn't need to be measurable
- **Key Results (KR):** "How do we know we're getting there?" — objectively measurable

### Typical Company-Level OKRs

- **Profits:** Massively grow revenues while minimizing expense
- **Product:** Delight our customers
- **People:** Create positive, transparent environment inspiring best work

### Formation Process

Each leadership team member separately writes proposed company OKRs → combine → debate → CEO decides (not consensus, but with buy-in from hearing everyone).

### Admin Week Timeline

- Monday: Leadership creates company OKRs (2 hrs, structured brainstorming)
- Tuesday: Departments create OKRs (2 hrs), CEO approves by EOD
- Wednesday: Teams create OKRs (2 hrs), dept heads approve by EOD
- Thursday: Sub-teams create OKRs (2 hrs), team heads approve by EOD
- Friday: Individuals create OKRs, sub-team heads approve by EOD
- Following Monday: CEO checks all, announces at All Hands

Keep the entire process to one week or less.

### Tracking

Traffic-light system (Green/Yellow/Red) in a Sheet or tool (15Five, Betterworks). Weekly updates. All Red OKRs require a written Issue/Solution from the DRI.

## 7. Accountability Partner

Doc: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1ljixCUhnIzKgNLzn5xzn-S1XEojM5Rblq3-83wiku28

### The Problem

Many people (especially extroverts) hate specific tasks (usually admin) and don't do them, creating debt. Delegation helps, but some tasks can't be delegated.

### Why Extroverts Struggle

Extroverts get energized by being around humans or doing fun things. Unfun tasks + alone = painful. But unfun tasks + another human present = fine.

### Solution

Ask your EA (or anyone) to simply be present (phone, video, or in-person) while you do the task. If no EA is available, use **Focusmate** — surprisingly effective.

## 8. Goals: How to Create and Review

Doc: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1S5ak38pEg7fZE5H2INOzUHHZJTW9QAB8aobQJW5Uyek

### Creating Goals

1. **Outcome-first:** "What outcome would make me say 'I crushed it in my role'?"
2. **Pick a timeframe:** Week, month, quarter, or year. Shorter = more action-oriented (good). Recommend 30-day goals.
3. **Prioritize:** If you could only get one done, which is it? That's #1.
4. **Limit:** Never more than 3 work goals + 1 personal. Best: track only 1 goal.

### Creating Next Actions

Goals are **outputs** (things that happen in the world). Next actions must be **inputs** (things you entirely control).

| Type | Example | You Control It? |
|---|---|---|
| Output | "Close X customer" | ❌ Customer decides |
| Input | "Send proposal to X customer" | ✅ Entirely you |

Always write next actions as inputs. This ensures you're never waiting on someone else.

### Make Timeframes as Short as Possible

Break actions down to what you can do in the next 2 minutes:
- "Send a proposal to X customer" (requires 2 hours) → 
- "Schedule 2 hours to write and send the proposal" (takes < 2 minutes, do it NOW)

Once an action is < 2 minutes, do it immediately. The flywheel of momentum begins. This is **bias to action**.
