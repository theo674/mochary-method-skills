## Core Thesis

When you feel fear or anger, your brain gives you bad advice. The prefrontal cortex (creative thought, problem-solving) produces your best thinking. The amygdala ("reptile brain") generates fear and anger and produces fight-or-flight responses that served survival in ancient times but create massive opportunity cost in the modern world.

## The Neuroscience (simplified)

Per John Haidt's "The Righteous Mind": human thought starts with emotion. We react with a feeling, then our brain constructs a story to explain it. Confirmation bias hardens the story into "truth."

Fear and anger are intelligent *signals*:
- **Fear** signals something new or risky (useful awareness)
- **Anger** signals something potentially harmful (useful awareness)

But that's where their intelligence ends. The *stories* fear and anger create are almost always wrong predictions. Evolutionarily, these false alarms kept humans alive when risk meant death. Today, they just prevent action and preserve ego.

## Two Recommendations

When you notice fear or anger:

### Option 1: Shift Out Before Acting

Take no action while in fear/anger. Wait until you've shifted into a calm state, then decide. (This is almost always the right move since urgency is almost never real.)

### Option 2: Do the Exact Opposite

If urgency genuinely requires immediate action:

- **In Anger** (brain says "crush this person"): Instead, get curious about why they did what they did. This doesn't mean you don't end the relationship later. It means you first genuinely seek to understand. There are lessons there because you likely had a hand in creating the situation.

- **In Fear** (brain says "don't do this scary thing"): Instead, do the action (in a kind way). The benefit likely outweighs the risk, and the unwanted outcome is probably not as dire as you imagine.

## The Self-Awareness Problem

"When I get angry, I just act. I only realize it much later."

This is the core challenge. Two solutions:

### Method 1: Meditation (Hard Way)
Meditate daily for years. Over time, the gap between feeling the emotion and recognizing it shrinks. Works, but slow and demanding.

### Method 2: Outsource Awareness (Easy Way)
Ask people around you (spouse, children, colleagues) to signal when they perceive you to be in fear or anger. This is dramatically faster and more effective.

**Critical: the exact phrasing matters.**

| Phrasing | Effect |
|---|---|
| "You're in anger" | Triggers more anger |
| "Are you in anger?" | Triggers more anger |
| **"I perceive you to be in anger"** | **Works. Allows the person to stop.** |

The key is "I perceive" — it's an observation, not an accusation. It doesn't threaten, so the person can actually hear it.

From the Loom, Matt emphasizes: "When my wife says 'I perceive you to be in anger,' I can immediately stop. With any other phrasing, I just feel more anger."

## Fear Journaling (from Regina Gerbeaux, COO at Mochary Method)

If no one is available to make bets with:

1. Create a Fear Journal
2. Whenever you feel fear (or someone points it out), write down the prediction your brain is making
3. Do this regularly to accumulate predictions
4. A week/month/year later, review the predictions
5. See how many actually came true
6. For those that did, ask if the outcome was actually negative

Prediction: very few will come true. Those that do will turn out net positive.

## The Bet Framework

To test this empirically: next time you're in fear, make a bet with someone. Your brain predicts Y will happen. Their brain predicts Z (likely the opposite). Do the action, see what happens.

Matt: "I have made these bets hundreds of times with people I perceive to be in fear. I have yet to lose."
