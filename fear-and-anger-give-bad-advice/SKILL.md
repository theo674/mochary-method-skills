# Fear and Anger Give Bad Advice

Coach someone through recognizing and managing decisions made from fear or anger, using the Mochary Method framework.

## When to Apply

- Someone is about to act from anger (fire someone, send a harsh message, confront aggressively)
- Someone is avoiding action out of fear (giving feedback, having a hard conversation, making a bold move)
- A person says "I just reacted" or regrets an impulsive decision
- Someone wants to build self-awareness around emotional triggers
- Debriefing a situation where fear/anger led to a bad outcome

## Core Principle

For the full framework with neuroscience context and tools, read `references/fear-anger-method.md`.

### Quick Reference

**The rule:** When in fear or anger, your brain gives you bad advice. Always.

**Two options:**
1. **Shift out first, then decide.** Take no action while triggered. (Almost always the right call since real urgency is rare.)
2. **Do the exact opposite.** Anger says "crush them" → get curious instead. Fear says "don't do it" → do it (kindly).

**The self-awareness problem:** You can't perceive your own fear/anger in the moment.

**Solution: outsource it.** Ask people around you to signal you. The exact words matter:
- ❌ "You're in anger" → triggers more anger
- ❌ "Are you in anger?" → triggers more anger
- ✅ **"I perceive you to be in anger"** → allows the person to stop

## How to Coach With This

### When someone is about to act from anger:
1. Name it: "I perceive you might be in anger right now"
2. Suggest pausing: "What if we wait until this shifts before deciding?"
3. If they must act now: "What's the opposite of what your brain is telling you to do?"
4. Redirect to curiosity: "What if you got curious about why they did what they did?"

### When someone is avoiding action from fear:
1. Name the fear: "What's the prediction your brain is making about what will happen?"
2. Reality-check: "How often do those predictions actually come true?"
3. Suggest the Fear Journal: write down the prediction, review it later
4. The bet: "Would you bet money on that prediction? I'd bet against it."
5. Encourage action: "What would it look like to do the thing, but kindly?"

### Building ongoing self-awareness:
1. Recommend outsourcing: ask 2-3 trusted people to signal when they perceive fear/anger
2. Give them the exact phrase: "I perceive you to be in [anger/fear]"
3. Start a Fear Journal: log predictions when triggered, review monthly
4. Track the hit rate: almost none of the feared outcomes materialize

## Gotchas

- **Phrasing is everything.** "You're in anger" and "Are you in anger?" both trigger more anger. Only "I perceive you to be in anger" works. Don't improvise.
- **"Do the opposite" doesn't mean be reckless.** It means get curious (anger) or take action kindly (fear). Not "blow up your life."
- **Real urgency is almost never real.** People in fear/anger think they must act NOW. They almost never do. Default to waiting.
- **Don't argue the neuroscience.** If someone pushes back, suggest the bet framework: write down fear's prediction, see if it comes true. Evidence beats debate.
- **You can't see your own fear/anger in the moment.** That's the whole problem. The solution is always outsourcing awareness, not willpower.
