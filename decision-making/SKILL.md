# Mochary Method: Decision-Making

Comprehensive decision-making frameworks from the Mochary Method. For full detail on all frameworks, read `references/decision-making-full.md`.

## Framework Selection Guide

Match the situation to the right framework:

| Situation | Framework |
|---|---|
| Stuck on a hard decision, everything feels bad | **Separate Decision from Implementation** |
| Need team buy-in on a decision | **Three Buy-In Methods** (pick by significance) |
| Someone bringing up a problem without a solution | **IPS Format** |
| Deciding who should make a decision and how fast | **Type 1 vs Type 2** |
| Too many people involved, can't reach consensus | **RAPID** |
| Leader is bottleneck on all decisions | **"I Intend To..."** |
| Leader's opinion is biasing the group | **Loudest Voice in the Room** |

## Quick Reference

### Separate Decision from Implementation
When a decision feels impossible, you're probably co-mingling the decision with the painful implementation. Separate them:
1. "Who do we serve primarily?" (customer, LP, citizen, family)
2. "What's in their best interest?" → that's the decision
3. Now think about implementation separately

Fear about implementation infects the decision. A thought partner who isn't in fear can walk you through it.

### "I Intend To..."
Replace "What should I do?" with **"I intend to [action]. Here's my reasoning."** Leader shifts from decider to reviewer. Team acts even if leader is unavailable. Removes bottleneck, trains decision-making muscle.

### Three Buy-In Methods
- **Method 1 (Announce):** Decide → announce → answer questions. Fast, low buy-in. Use for low-impact decisions.
- **Method 2 (Straw Man):** Write a proposed answer → share → collect feedback → discuss → decide. Good balance. Use for most decisions.
- **Method 3 (Brainstorm):** Team brainstorms from scratch → leader speaks last → leader writes proposal → feedback → decide. Maximum buy-in. Use for major decisions (vision, roadmap, goals).

### IPS Format
Every issue brought to a meeting must be in writing:
1. **Issue** — what's the problem
2. **What I did to create this** — removes blame, reveals self-solve path
3. **Proposed Solution** — bold and directive, even at 10% confidence

### Type 1 vs Type 2 (Bezos)
- **Type 1:** Irreversible, one-way door → decide carefully
- **Type 2:** Reversible, two-way door → decide fast, delegate to reports
- "Decisions are almost never Type 1." — Mike Vernal (Sequoia)

### RAPID
For large groups or no consensus in 5-20 min. Roles: Recommend, Agree, Perform, Input, Decide. See `references/decision-making-full.md` for the complete process.

### Loudest Voice in the Room
CEO's opinion biases the group. Solution: have everyone write their thoughts or vote simultaneously BEFORE the leader shares. Junior people speak first (Amazon practice). Leader speaks last.

## Coaching Application

When helping someone with a decision:
1. First, check: are they stuck because of fear about implementation? → Separate the two
2. Classify: Type 1 or Type 2? (Almost certainly Type 2 → move fast, consider delegating)
3. How much buy-in is needed? → Pick Method 1, 2, or 3
4. If the team is large or stuck → set up a RAPID
5. If the leader is the bottleneck → introduce "I intend to..."
6. If the leader's voice is dominating → apply Loudest Voice techniques

## Gotchas

- **"I feel lost" usually means decision and implementation are tangled.** Separate them. The decision is almost always clear once you ask "What's in the best interest of who we serve?"
- **Most decisions are Type 2.** If someone is agonizing, ask: "Is this reversible?" If yes, move fast and delegate.
- **Method 3 is overkill for most decisions.** Only use full brainstorm-from-scratch for vision, roadmap, and goals. Method 2 (straw man) covers 80% of important decisions.
- **IPS proposed solutions must be bold.** Even at 10% confidence, write it as "Do this." A hedge like "maybe we could consider..." kills the discussion.
- **RAPID fails without written prep.** If people show up to a Decision Meeting without having written their input, send them back. Verbal-only RAPID is just another meeting.
- **"I intend to..." requires the leader to actually let go.** If the leader always overrides, the team stops bringing well-thought-out decisions.
