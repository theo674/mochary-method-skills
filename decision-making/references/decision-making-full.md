## 1. Separate Decision from Implementation

Doc: https://docs.google.com/document/d/18VkFRbhDTeU0nBEiot8CXiK37UUEZp0D-sT4-IN02Hs

(Credit: Wei Deng of Clipboard Health shared this rubric.)

### The Problem

When facing difficult decisions, we feel lost because we're co-mingling the *decision* and the *implementation*. The decision itself is often clear, but the implementation feels painful. Fear about implementation infects our view of the decision.

### The Process

1. **Ask "Who do we serve primarily?"**
   - Company → your customer
   - Fund → CEOs you invest in + LPs
   - Government → citizens
   - Family → family members (including you)

2. **Ask "What is in the best interest of the person we serve primarily?"**
   - This answer IS the correct decision. Write it down.

3. **Now think about implementation separately.** Two routes:
   - Simply implement without worrying about injury it causes, OR
   - Look at the injury it causes to others → reach out to those people → discover what really matters to them → think of ways to help them achieve that → then implement

4. **If you can't follow this in practice:** ask a co-worker, spouse, or friend to be your thought partner. Share the framework. They can walk you through it because *they* are not in fear about the pain of implementation. Only you are.

## 2. Turn the Ship Around: "I Intend To..."

Doc: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1Uf5W4C0KG6Ywgsnh0TFlNbZTGymdgeTODAkpBYfj6Bo

From L. David Marquet's book *Turn the Ship Around*.

### The Story

Marquet was assigned to command the Navy's worst-performing submarine, a completely different class than he'd trained on. He couldn't make the decisions because he didn't know how anything operated. Out of necessity, department heads made decisions. He simply asked them to say **"I intend to..."** before acting, so he could ask questions or give feedback.

### What Happened

- If Marquet wasn't available, departments acted anyway (no bottleneck)
- Department heads started coming earlier with well-thought-out decisions
- They began proactively presenting cross-department impact before being asked
- Each department head started thinking like a commander

### Results

- The sub went from worst to best-performing in the fleet
- Went from lowest to highest promotion rate (department heads were training themselves for command every time they said "I intend to...")

### Matt's Application

Matt uses this inside the Mochary Method team. Results: he feels fully informed, is no longer the bottleneck, and quality of decisions, actions, and buy-in have gone up dramatically. CEOs he coaches report the same.

### How to Implement

Replace "What should I do?" with **"I intend to [action]. Here's my reasoning: [context]."**

The leader's role shifts from decision-maker to reviewer/questioner. If the leader is unavailable, the team acts anyway.

## 3. Getting Buy-In: Three Methods

Doc: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1hPdWQjYfdK0SB2i3ViZ9XsMPjTYEi7Do1WI22ofmEdo

### The Core Challenge

It's often easy to decide, but hard to get the team emotionally invested. Buy-in comes from people feeling they are part of the decision and their input contributes to the outcome.

### Method 1: Announce and Answer
- Decision-maker decides, announces to team, answers questions
- **Pro:** Fast
- **Con:** Minimal buy-in, ignores collective knowledge

### Method 2: Straw Man + Feedback
- Create a written straw man (hypothetical answer to inspire discussion)
- Share with team, invite written and verbal feedback
- Facilitate group discussion, make everyone feel heard
- Decision-maker decides
- **Pro:** More buy-in, some benefit from collective wisdom
- **Con:** More time

### Method 3: Brainstorm from Scratch
- Team meets and discusses with no straw man
- Team shares ideas via structured brainstorming
- Decision-maker repeats everyone's ideas until heard (shares hers last — see "Loudest Voice" below)
- Decision-maker asynchronously creates proposed solution
- Shares for feedback, then decides (NOT consensus)
- **Pro:** Most buy-in, most benefit from collective wisdom
- **Con:** Most time

### Which Method?

| Decision Significance | Method |
|---|---|
| Low-impact (holiday party venue) | Method 1 |
| Medium importance (most decisions) | Method 2 |
| Major/core (10-year vision, goals, roadmap) | Method 3 |

## 4. Issues and Proposed Solutions (IPS)

When bringing up an issue, require it in writing:
1. **Issue** — detailed description
2. **What I did to create this issue** — removes blame, often reveals self-solve path
3. **Proposed Solution** — bold, directive terms ("Do this...") even with 10% confidence

Present at weekly Team Meeting. Allow 5 min for tactical, 20 min for strategic. If decision-maker has enough context, decide and assign Next Action with DRI + Due Date. If not, use RAPID.

## 5. Type 1 vs Type 2 Decisions

From Jeff Bezos's 2016 Shareholder Letter:

- **Type 1** — Consequential and irreversible (one-way doors). Make methodically, carefully, slowly.
- **Type 2** — Changeable, reversible (two-way doors). Make quickly by high-judgment individuals or small groups.

**Key insight from Mike Vernal (Sequoia, ex-Facebook VP):** "Decisions are almost never Type 1."

For Type 2: let a report be the Decision-Maker. Faster decision, exercises their decision-making muscle, builds your confidence in them.

## 6. RAPID Decision-Making

From Bain Consulting, introduced to Matt by Emilie Choi (President, Coinbase). Use when:
- Team is too large to get all voices in one room
- Team cannot reach consensus in 5-20 minutes

### Roles

| Role | Who | Responsibility |
|---|---|---|
| **R** (Recommend) | Proposer of issue/solution | Writes issue + proposed solution, solicits input |
| **A** (Agree) | Colleagues whose input is vital (often Legal) | Must agree for decision to proceed |
| **P** (Perform) | Those who will enact the decision | Should contribute since they implement |
| **I** (Input) | Those whose input is worth considering | Provide perspective |
| **D** (Decide) | The decision-maker | Makes final call |

### Process

1. R writes Issue + Proposed Solution + lists all R/A/P/I/D people
2. R solicits input from A, P, I people (written on the document)
3. R schedules Decision Meeting (urgent = ASAP, non-urgent = next Team Meeting)
4. At meeting: D reads document, asks questions
5. If answerable in 5 min → D decides
6. If not → another round of written responses → decide at next meeting
7. D writes up Decision + Next Actions (each with DRI + Due Date) → publishes to company

### Supporting Docs

- **RAPID Tracker:** Sheet tracking each RAPID (link, R, D, meeting date, completion)
- **RAPID Feedback:** Doc for Like/Wish That feedback after each Decision Meeting

## 7. Loudest Voice in the Room

Doc: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1OKJnyxUZDOcPQF0VtERizzyvKgBiqwOA7aL2dMcFSdw

(Inspired by Anthony Ghosn, CEO of Hearth.)

### The Problem

As CEO, your voice is the "loudest in the room." Once people hear your perspective, a surprisingly high percentage will alter their views to match yours. They assume you have more information. Later, they won't feel fully bought-in because internally they know their true thought wasn't heard.

### The Solution

Don't tip your hand before others share theirs:
- Have people **write down** their vote/thoughts before you share
- Or use **simultaneous thumbs-up/thumbs-down** voting
- Use **structured written brainstorming** (3 minutes, everyone writes)

### Supporting Evidence

- **Cuban Missile Crisis (13 Days, RFK):** Classic example of this dynamic and the solution
- **Amazon practice (from Peter Reinhardt, Segment):** Most junior people speak and ask questions first. Also surfaces junior talent and gives seniors a chance to observe.

### Key Principle

Share your perspective LAST. The more influence people feel toward the outcome, the more invested they'll be.
