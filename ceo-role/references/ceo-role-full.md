# CEO Role: Full Reference

Source: Mochary Method (Matt Mochary)

## 1. Mission, Vision, Values: How to Create Them

Doc: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1L6ks72TkBIlgPDh6_n-NBBE7e_7nQ2YZ6B4BF12IQEs

### Mission

One simple, short, memorable sentence answering: "Our purpose is to do **what** for **whom**?"

### Vision (10-Year)

Imagine it's 10 years from now and you dominate the industry. Answer:
1. What industry do we dominate?
2. Who is our customer? (a real human, not a corporate entity)
3. What pain do we solve for them?
4. What's unique about our solution that makes customers choose us?
5. What asset (human or physical) makes it difficult for competitors to copy us? (the moat)

### Values

Values define behaviors you want universally. Hire for them, fire for unwillingness to exhibit them.

**Method 1 (aspirational, for small companies):** Complete: "The rest of you can make all decisions from now on, as long as you …"

**Method 2 (culture-acknowledging, for sizable teams):** Written brainstorm: each leader names a non-leader in the company who exhibits a behavior they wish were universal. Name the person AND the behavior. Select 3-5 examples. These become your values with real examples.

## 2. Top Goal (CEO)

Doc: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1rWlx4P0Kpp4E8nFajcwDa4or_1EINv_ITTM-Ruo5XvQ
Loom: https://www.loom.com/share/ad69bd9f2100436f8940fa9d275d37b7 (2 min, Matt Mochary)

From Greg McKeown's *Essentialism*: **Schedule two hours each day to work on your Top Goal only.** Every single workday. During this time, do not respond to emails, texts, calls, or messages.

### Key Details

- Schedule it early in the day (brain is freshest, fewer interruptions)
- If two hours feels too much, start with 30 minutes. Increase weekly until you reach two hours.
- Research shows we have more decision and thought-processing energy early in the day

### From the Loom

Matt shares his coaching experience: after the first meeting, he and the CEO identify the most important actions. By the second meeting, if they haven't done them, it's almost always because of inbound requests. The fix: carve out Top Goal time. If by the third meeting they still haven't done their actions, Matt stops coaching them. It's that fundamental.

## 3. CEO Role

Doc: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1yJhwYFf6Af0h8_xMDuKeW8d6ZomthidraqpXHI9RKRw

### Typical CEO Responsibilities

1. Set and communicate vision, mission, values, quarterly goals
2. Ensure product solves current and future customer problems
3. Create and maintain relationships with investors, customers, partners, employees
4. Report to board and investors
5. Hire and manage exec team
6. Hold regular 1-1s and team meetings
7. Cause departments to create goals (department → team → individual)
8. Hold people accountable for quarterly goals
9. Maintain high hiring bar consistent with values
10. Ensure resource allocation by priority and merit (not squeaky wheel)
11. Put out fires at department fault lines
12. Run any department without a head

### What Cannot Be Outsourced

- Relationships with key stakeholders (employees, partners, customers, investors)
- Board/investor reporting
- Hiring the exec team
- Future visioning of company and products

### What Can (and Should) Be Outsourced

Everything else. The CEO should only do the functions she loves. The rest goes to someone who does love them — typically an Exec Ops/CoS. After 90 days of shadowing, they can take over any role the CEO doesn't love.

### When to Fire Yourself as CEO

If you're completely burned out, and the things you don't love include people management, hiring, sales, and fundraising (you only love Product): hire a CEO, name yourself Chairman. You keep your equity, it becomes more valuable, and you build your ideal life.

## 4. Zone of Genius

Doc: https://docs.google.com/document/d/11UVWJox31Ani8Cq6Ymhm6DiVUiv5vPkOQd8ZdGpYmbs

### The Four Zones

| Zone | Description | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Incompetence** | Others do it better | Outsource (unless it gives you joy) |
| **Competence** | You're fine, others are equally good | Outsource (unless it gives you joy) |
| **Excellence** | You're better than others, but don't love it | **Danger zone.** People want you to keep doing it. Move away from it. |
| **Genius** | Uniquely good + you love it (time/space disappear) | Spend most/all time here |

### False Fear

"If everyone only does their Zone of Genius, who does the unfun stuff?" False. For every task you find unfun, someone else excels at it AND loves it (yes, even firing people). Map activities to right people via AORs.

### How to Discover Your Zone of Genius

You probably can't see it yourself (time/space disappear when you're in it). Crowdsource it. Send this to people who interact with you regularly:

> "I'm trying to understand what I'm uniquely good at. Would you take 10 minutes to answer:
> 1. What do I do that you experience as 'world-class'?
> 2. What do I do where I appear to experience fun, peace, or joy?
> 3. What do I do that I'm good at but don't appear to enjoy?
> 4. What do I do that I'm not better than others?
> 5. What do I do that I'm actually worse than others?"

### Naval Ravikant on Zone of Genius

YouTube: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WJuQLzr6PJQ (22 min)

Naval's key story: as CEO of AngelList, his life was "3 out of 10, sometimes 2." After stepping back and recruiting Avlok Kohli as CEO of AngelList Venture, his life became "10 out of 10 across the board." His zone of genius was creativity, strategy, high-level sales, and recruiting. Everything else went to operators who loved those functions.

Key insights from Naval:
- **Empty calendar = creativity.** "A busy calendar is the death of creativity." He carved out two meeting-free days per week as CEO. Even then, one would get disrupted.
- **Compound returns come at the end.** You need to sustain engagement for decades. Only possible if you enjoy what you do.
- **Recruit failed founders.** Find founders whose companies failed because the category failed (not execution). They have founder mentality, operating experience, and a chip on their shoulder. Give them peer status and generous equity.
- **Don't keep a calendar.** After stepping back, Naval stopped keeping a calendar entirely. "People don't understand the value of free time for a creative and productive person."
- **Walking away enables walking away from anything.** The fear of stepping back is real (Naval felt it for a year), but once you do it, the freedom compounds.

## 5. Exec Ops (formerly Chief of Staff)

Doc: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1-tcubUwvktoV-Ou3bB6ynk2LLwNzdT3D_p_ILreyR_A

### When to Hire

If you're not completing your weekly goals, or tasks are energy-draining. Matt now requires this hire before coaching anyone. It should be the very first hire.

### Three Versions

| Version | Profile | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| **Junior** | Wants to support, no department ambition | Super-charged EA, stays long-term |
| **Senior** | Wants to eventually run a department | Takes over ailing departments, becomes de facto COO |
| **Very Senior** | 10-15 years experience, execs will accept reporting to them | Strategic thought-partner, crafts future vision, becomes COO |

Eventually you'll want both a senior version (runs departments) and a junior version (stays in Exec Ops permanently).

### Background

Highly organized, excellent written and oral communicator, and you like spending time with them. For senior: broad strategic business knowledge (ideal: several years at Bain/BCG/McKinsey). 1-2 years in tech is nice-to-have.

### Training (Critical)

Give them **unfettered access to all your information**: email, calls, meetings. Have them sit beside you morning to night. By seeing what decisions you make based on what information, they learn to think like you.

- First 3-6 months: in-person shadowing (even if remote company)
- After that: remote shadow via open Zoom with screen share
- After several weeks: start taking tasks off your plate
- Within several months: can do all functions that don't give you energy

### Key Rules

- **Never let them be a gatekeeper.** They can draft emails on your behalf, but never intercept and respond themselves.
- **Don't wait for the perfect person.** Pick someone good, start the 30-day shadowing clock immediately.
- **When it's working (2-3 months), immediately hire 1-2 more.** They become "Members of the Office of the CEO" — your smokejumpers. Never be left without 1-2 available.

### The Two Types of Exec Ops (Recruiting Pitch)

- **Glorified gopher** (bad): No email access, no meeting shadowing, does orphan projects. Dead-end role, repels strong candidates.
- **Full access** (good): Sees all CEO information, shadows all meetings. Most intense CEO training that exists. Attracts strong candidates because it leads to running departments or becoming de facto COO.

### Scaling Warning

Once the Exec Ops starts running a department, they stop taking admin off your plate. Life gets worse. Solution: have them hire 1-2 more Exec Ops before they transition. Always have bench depth.

### Testimonials

- **Francis Davidson (CEO, Sonder):** CoS runs all OKRs, roadmap planning, dispatches a 10-person team into struggling departments. "His team ensures we are a well-run company and I'm not dependent on making that happen."
- **Chris Mansi (CEO, Viz.ai):** Promoted EA to CoS without typical GSB/McKinsey background. "Trust is important as they'll be in every meeting, including comp and challenging conversations."
