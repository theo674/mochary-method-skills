# Mochary Heard Method

Guide someone through the "Heard" technique from the Mochary Method. This is the single most powerful tool for creating trust and connection.

## When to Apply

- Someone is giving or receiving difficult feedback
- A person feels unheard or resentful
- Coaching someone before/during/after a tense 1:1
- Preparing for a conversation where emotions will run high
- Someone asks how to be a better listener or build trust

## Core Flow

For the full method with examples, read `references/heard-method.md`.

### Quick Reference

1. **Prepare:** Glass case visualization. Nothing they say hits you. If you get triggered, you can't make them feel heard.
2. **Listen:** Full presence. No distractions. Intention is to understand, not respond.
3. **Acknowledge:**
   - *Feedback/anger:* "I think I heard you say …" → "Is that right?" → "Is there more?"
   - *Sharing pain:* "I imagine you are feeling …" → "Is that right?" → "There's more. Please tell me more."
4. **Confirm:** "Do you feel heard?"

### Key Principles

- Acknowledging is more impactful than solving the problem or acting on feedback
- When reflecting back, amp up the emotion (they've toned it down out of fear)
- Keep going deeper until they say "No, that's it" — there is almost always more
- If someone won't share, imagine their thoughts and say them: "I imagine you're thinking …"

## How to Coach With This

When helping someone prepare for or debrief a conversation:

1. Identify the situation type (feedback, conflict, someone sharing pain)
2. Walk them through the preparation step (glass case, expect to feel triggered)
3. Role-play the acknowledgment phrases for their specific situation
4. Remind them: the goal is "Do you feel heard?" — not fixing, not defending, not solving
5. After the conversation, ask what happened and whether the other person felt heard

## Reverse Application

When the person themselves doesn't feel heard, coach them to ask:
"To make sure I'm communicating well, can you please repeat back what you heard me say?"

## Gotchas

- **Don't skip the glass case step.** If the listener gets triggered, the entire technique backfires. Preparation is not optional.
- **Don't tone it down when reflecting back.** People have already softened what they really think. If you soften further, they won't feel heard. Amp it up.
- **"Is that right?" is not rhetorical.** Actually wait for confirmation. If they say "not quite," keep going until you get it right.
- **Don't jump to solving.** Acknowledging is more powerful than fixing. The urge to solve is the #1 reason this technique fails.
- **Path A vs Path B matters.** Feedback/anger → repeat their words back. Sharing pain → imagine their feelings. Using the wrong path feels hollow.
- **"Do you feel heard?" is the final check.** Don't assume. Ask explicitly.
