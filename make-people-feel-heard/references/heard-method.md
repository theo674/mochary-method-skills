# Heard: How to Make People Feel It

Source: Mochary Method (Matt Mochary)

## Core Principle

When people have a feeling or thought, they want to feel heard. If they don't, they will feel resentment. Making someone feel heard is more impactful than acting on their feedback or helping them solve their problem.

## The Process

### Step 0: Prepare (Glass Case)

Before listening, tell yourself: if anger, fear, or sadness come up, they will give you bad advice. Imagine a glass case around the other person. Nothing they say leaves that glass case. None of it hits you.

This is critical. If you get triggered, you cannot make the person feel heard. You will do the opposite.

### Step 1: Ask and Listen

Ask the person to express their feelings and thoughts. Be fully present. Put away distractions. Your entire intention is to listen to understand, not to respond.

### Step 2: Acknowledge

Two paths depending on the situation:

#### Path A: Feedback or Anger Directed at You

1. Say: "I think I heard you say …" and summarize what they said
2. Ask: "Is that right?"
3. If "Yes" → done (or go deeper)
4. If "Not quite" → ask them to repeat, summarize again until correct
5. Go deeper: "Is there more?"
6. Repeat until they say "No, that's it"

**Advanced level:** When repeating back, describe the feeling they're experiencing. Exaggerate the thoughts. Use strong language. They've toned down what they really think out of fear you'll get angry. If you tone it down further, they won't feel heard. If you amp it up, they'll say "it's not that extreme" but they WILL feel heard.

**Example:**
- What was said: "I wish that you wouldn't act in anger."
- What's likely in their head: "Goddammit, why do you always have to be such an f-ing bully? Let people talk and at least have the courtesy to not rage at the ones who are telling you what you need to hear while the others cower in silence!"

#### Path B: Sharing Something Painful

1. Don't repeat back what they said
2. Instead say: "I imagine that you are feeling …" and honestly try to imagine their feelings
3. Ask: "Is that right?"
4. Go deeper: "Is there more?" or better: "There's more. Please tell me more."
5. Repeat until complete

### Step 3: Confirm

Ask: "Do you feel heard?"

### When Someone Won't Share

If someone won't say what they're feeling:
1. Imagine what they're thinking
2. Say: "I imagine that you are thinking something like …"
3. Ask: "Is that right?"
4. If "No" → ask what their thoughts are (now they've seen you won't get angry, they're more likely to share)
5. If still won't share → try imagining again, repeat

### Reverse: When YOU Don't Feel Heard

Ask the other person: "To make sure I'm communicating well, can you please repeat back what you heard me say?"

If that feels too blunt, share this framework with them first.
